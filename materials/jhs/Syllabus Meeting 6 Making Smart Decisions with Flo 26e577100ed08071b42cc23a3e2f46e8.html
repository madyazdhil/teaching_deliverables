Topic 1: A Quick Refresher on Our Tools

Mission Briefing: Let's Revisit Our Flowchart Basics!

Last time, we learned that a flowchart is a visual map of an algorithm. We used our first set of symbols to plan a simple process. Do you remember these?

  • Terminator (Oval): Used for the Start and End of our process.
  • Process (Rectangle): Used for any action or command, like "Add jelly to bread."
  • Input/Output (Parallelogram): We also learned this shape for whenever our process needs to get information (input) or display information (output).
    • Input Example: "Ask for user's name."
    • Output Example: "Display 'Hello!' message."

Today, we're focusing on a powerful new symbol that makes our flowcharts "smart."

Topic 2: The Power of Choice: Introducing the Decision Symbol

Making Your Flowchart "Smart" 🧠

What makes a process interesting? The ability to make choices! In a flowchart, we use the Decision (Diamond) symbol to ask a question.

A Decision diamond always asks a question that can be answered with "Yes" or "No" (or "True"/"False"). Because there are two possible answers, there will always be two arrows coming out of a diamond, one for each path.

This allows our flowchart to change its behavior based on a condition.

Case Study #1: The Umbrella Dilemma ☔

Problem: Should you take an umbrella when you leave the house? The answer depends on the weather.

The Algorithm:

  1. Start
  1. Look outside.
  1. Is it raining?
  1. If YES, take an umbrella.
  1. If NO, wear sunglasses.
  1. End

Topic 4: The Never-Ending Story: How Loops Work

What is a Loop? 🔁

Does a traffic light change colors once and then stop forever? No! It repeats its pattern over and over. This is a loop. A loop is a way to repeat a set of actions.

In a flowchart, we show a loop simply by drawing a flow line (arrow) from a later step back to an earlier step. This creates a cycle that can repeat.

Brainstorming Session: What Else is a Loop? In small groups, brainstorm things in real life that repeat in a cycle. Be ready to share!

  • Example 1: A washing machine's cycle (wash, rinse, spin, repeat?).
  • Example 2: Brushing your teeth (up, down, up, down...).

Topic 5: The Ultimate Flowchart Challenge: Traffic Light Logic

Your Mission: Control the Intersection! 🚦

It's time to combine everything: sequencing, decisions (implied by timers), and loops. Your challenge is to create a flowchart for a simple traffic light.

The Logic You Need to Map:

  1. Start
  1. Turn the main street light Green.
  1. Wait for 30 seconds.
  1. Turn the main street light Yellow.
  1. Wait for 5 seconds.
  1. Turn the main street light Red.
  1. Loop! Go back to step 2 and repeat the cycle forever.

Instructions:

  • Use a printed template or a digital tool like Draw.io.
  • Start with a Terminator for "Start".
  • Use Process rectangles for all the actions (like "Turn light Green" or "Wait 30s").
  • Connect the steps in the correct sequence with arrows.
  • To create the loop, draw your final arrow from the last step all the way back to the beginning of the cycle (Step 2).

Solo Missions: Create Your Own Flowcharts

Now it's your turn to solve some problems by creating a flowchart for each case study. Use the symbols you've learned: Terminator, Process, Input/Output, and Decision.

Case Study #1: The Gaming Session You want to play a video game, but your rule is that you can only play if your homework is done. Create a flowchart that decides whether you should play the game or do your homework.

Case Study #2: The Snack Machine A snack machine sells chips for Rp 5,000. Create a flowchart that takes a user's money as input, checks if they inserted enough money, and then either dispenses the chips or tells them they don't have enough money.

Case Study #3: The Popcorn Master You are making microwave popcorn. The instructions say to stop the microwave when the popping slows down to less than one pop every two seconds. Create a flowchart with a loop that checks if the popping is slow. If it's not slow, it should keep waiting. If it is slow, it should stop the microwave.

Case Study #4: The Pet Feeder You are training your dog (or cat!). Before you give them their food, you want them to sit. Create a flowchart that tells the pet to sit. It should check if the pet sat. If the pet did not sit, it should loop and tell them to sit again. If the pet did sit, the flowchart should proceed to give them their food.
