Topic 2: Introducing Pseudocode - A Coder's Blueprint

What is this "Pseudocode" thing? 🤔

You've just seen that plain English can be a bit of a disaster for giving instructions. Pseudocode is our solution.

Analogy: Think of it like a middle ground. It's not as strict as a real programming language (like Python or JavaScript), but it's much more organized and clear than just talking. It's like a structured recipe that a computer can almost understand.

Today, you will learn to translate your flowchart "drawings" into these structured "recipes." This is the last step you take before writing real, working code.

Topic 3: Translating Your Flowchart Recipe

Teacher Demonstration: From a Sandwich Flowchart to Pseudocode

Let's take a simple, everyday task: making a sandwich.

Step 1: The Flowchart First, here is a simple flowchart for making a sandwich. Notice the different shapes for actions and decisions.

Step 2: The Translation Now, let's translate this flowchart into pseudocode, line-by-line. As we do this, we'll introduce some important keywords that give our "recipe" structure.

Flowchart SymbolPseudocode Keyword/StructureExample
Oval (Start/End)START / ENDSTART ... END
Rectangle (Process)A clear commandGet two slices of bread
Diamond (Decision)IF-THEN-ENDIFIF cheese is wanted THEN     Add a slice of cheese ENDIF
Diamond (Decision with a "No" path)IF-THEN-ELSE-ENDIFIF it is raining THEN     Take umbrella ELSE     Wear sunglasses ENDIF

Our Sandwich Pseudocode:

START
  Get two slices of bread
  Put filling on bread
  IF cheese is wanted THEN
    Add a slice of cheese
  ENDIF
  Put slices of bread together
END

Notice how the action "Add a slice of cheese" is indented. This is important! It shows that this step only happens if the IFcondition is true.

Topic 4: Your Turn - The Traffic Light Pseudocode

Your Mission: Translate Your Flowchart! 🚦

Now it's your turn to be the translator.

  1. Open your traffic light flowchart you created in Draw.io.
  1. In a new text document, translate your flowchart into pseudocode.
  1. Remember to use the keywords START, END, and clear commands for each step.
  1. Since your flowchart has a loop that goes on forever, you can use the keywords LOOP and ENDLOOP to show the repeating section.

Example Structure for a Loop:

START
  LOOP
    ... all the repeating steps go here ...
    ... like turning lights green, waiting, turning them yellow ...
  ENDLOOP
END

Topic 5: Logic Check, Discussion, and Quiz!

Pair-Check: Be the Logic Checker

Get into pairs with a classmate. Your job is to review your partner's pseudocode. You are a "logic checker," not a grammar teacher! Can you follow their instructions from start to finish?

Check for these three things:

  1. Clarity: Is each step a clear command? (e.g., "Turn light green" is better than "the light").
  1. Decision Logic (if you have any): Does every IF have a matching ENDIF? Is the action inside the IF indented correctly?
  1. Completeness: Does it have a START and an END?

Topic 7: Let’s Talk

Recap Discussion: What's the Point of Pseudocode?

Let's discuss as a class: Why do we do this? Why not just write the real code?

Key Takeaways:

  • It helps you plan your logic without worrying about forgetting a semicolon or a comma in a real language.
  • It's a universal language—anyone can understand it, even if they don't know Python or JavaScript.
  • It makes writing the real code much faster and easier because the plan is already perfect.
